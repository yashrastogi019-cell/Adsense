Notion vs Obsidian vs Roam — Which Note-Taking App Wins?

RA
Rasto
Published Apr 18, 2025

The Knowledge Management Battle

Personal knowledge management has become a discipline of its own. With information overload at an all-time high, the tools you use to capture, organize, and retrieve knowledge directly impact your productivity and thinking quality. Three applications have emerged as the leading contenders: Notion, Obsidian, and Roam Research. Each takes a fundamentally different approach to knowledge management.

Notion — The All-in-One Workspace

Notion combines notes, databases, project management, wikis, and collaboration into a single platform. Its block-based editor lets you build pages from modular components — text, tables, kanban boards, calendars, galleries, and more. For teams and individuals who want one tool for everything, Notion's versatility is unmatched.

The database feature is Notion's secret weapon. You can create relational databases, link entries across tables, create custom views with filters and sorts, and build dashboards that pull data from multiple sources. This makes Notion powerful for CRM, content calendars, habit tracking, and project management — use cases that other note-taking apps simply cannot handle.

However, Notion requires an internet connection for most operations. Offline support exists but is limited and unreliable. For users who need guaranteed access to their notes without connectivity, this is a significant limitation. Notion also stores your data on their servers, which raises privacy concerns for some users.

Obsidian — The Local-First Powerhouse

Obsidian takes the opposite approach: everything is stored locally as plain Markdown files on your device. This means your notes are future-proof, portable, and completely under your control. You can open them in any text editor, back them up however you choose, and never worry about a company shutting down and taking your data with it.

Obsidian's killer feature is its graph view and bidirectional linking system. As you create links between notes, Obsidian builds a visual knowledge graph that reveals connections and patterns you might not have noticed. For researchers, writers, and anyone building a personal knowledge base over years, this networked approach to note-taking is transformative.

The plugin ecosystem is another major strength. With over 1,000 community plugins, you can customize Obsidian to do almost anything — from Kanban boards to Dataview queries to Excalidraw whiteboarding. The trade-off is that achieving Notion-like functionality requires significant setup and configuration.

Roam Research — The Networked Thought Pioneer

Roam Research pioneered the concept of bidirectional linking and block-level references in note-taking. Every bullet point in Roam is individually addressable and can be referenced from any other page, creating a densely interconnected web of ideas. The daily notes feature encourages a journal-style approach where you capture thoughts chronologically and let the linking system create structure organically.

Roam's outliner-based approach is particularly popular among academics, researchers, and writers who think in hierarchical structures. The ability to embed and transclude blocks from one page into another means you can build arguments and documents by assembling ideas from across your entire knowledge base without duplicating content.

The main barriers to Roam are pricing ($15/month — the most expensive option) and a steep learning curve. The interface is minimal to the point of being intimidating for new users, and there is no free tier to experiment with.

Our Verdict

Choose Notion if you want an all-in-one workspace that combines notes, databases, and project management with excellent collaboration features. Choose Obsidian if you value data ownership, offline access, and want to build a long-term personal knowledge base with maximum customization. Choose Roam Research if networked thinking and block-level referencing are central to your research or writing workflow and you are willing to pay the premium price.

For most users, we recommend starting with Notion for its versatility and low learning curve, or Obsidian for its privacy-first approach and extensibility.
